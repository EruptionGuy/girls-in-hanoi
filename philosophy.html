The Ethics of Urban Form: Recognition and Absence in Hanoi's Streetscape

The Urban Language of Remembrance

Street naming is an urban language. Maps tell us who the city chooses to remember and whose labor is absorbed into the collective. When a woman’s name appears on a sign in Hanoi, it feels sacred—a rare moment when her invisible contribution becomes tangible.

The scarcity of these names, however, is equally revealing. It implies that women's stories have often been honored through the collective contributions—the tea, the rice, a sustained family—rather than as individual brilliance. Their endurance has built the very rhythm of Hanoi, but their legacy rarely earns a plaque.

The city’s architecture is not just French facades and colonial relics; it is also the invisible scaffolding of care that women maintain daily: the early market stalls, the patience that holds chaos at bay.

A Constellation of Strength

The few streets named after women embody this paradox: few, yet pulsing with life. They remind us that visibility is not about numbers, but about essence.

Hai Bà Trưng: A grand boulevard echoing the story of the twin sisters who led an uprising, their defiance spanning centuries.
Bà Triệu: A gentler street where her spirit of defiance lingers in the breeze.
Nguyễn Thị Minh Khai: Her name belongs to modernity, representing progress, independence, and education.

Together, they form a quiet constellation—a philosophy of female strength written into the city’s geography.

Beyond the Map: A Philosophy of Work

Girls in Hanoi was born from this observation, a form of listening to what is absent. My philosophy of work is rooted in presence and observation: to look at what is missing and recognize that absence itself is a powerful narrative. In a city where women’s names are so few on signs, my work seeks to create other forms of recognition—through narratives, images, and conversations.

Walking Bà Triệu Street, I see the philosophy of the feminine city: not fragile, but balanced; not ornamental, but fundamentally grounding. The street’s rhythm is deliberate and free, mirroring the cadence of women’s work—the steaming of rolls, the stitching of fabric, the quiet, sustaining thought. These are not small acts; they are the architecture of living.

Perhaps Hanoi has always understood this: women’s immortality is made of softer things—the scent of cốm, the sound of laughter, the patience that sustains life.

I don't dream of building new streets. I dream of naming the ones already there: the invisible roads women walk every day—between home and market, love and labor. Every woman is a street of her own, carrying stories and meanings that deserve to be mapped.

Ultimately, Hanoi teaches a simple lesson: presence is measured not by public acclaim, but by how deeply your work becomes an integral part of a place. To walk through this city is to walk through women’s work—patient, uncredited, and beautiful—the work that keeps a city alive.
