The Hands That Shape The World

Hanoi is built as much by policies as by palms — the hands that raise scaffolds, wash sidewalks, pass books, soothe children. My camera stayed close to those hands, as if tracing the heartbeat of urban life through its smallest motions. There’s something about Hanoi’s rhythm; it’s always moving, but never rushing. Morning markets, afternoon classrooms, evening homes. Their gestures repeat, but each repetition changes slightly, like the pulse of the city itself evolving gently through touch. It came to me how their hands contained both exhaustion and grace — a duality that modern cities often erase in their pursuit of efficiency. You could read entire histories in those movements: the decades of rebuilding after war, the inheritance of care, the quiet persistence that outlasts policies and skylines. A city doesn’t just expand upward economically or in any rigorous means, it expands through the reach of those who hold it together every single day.
In Hanoi, it’s by these women's hands.
